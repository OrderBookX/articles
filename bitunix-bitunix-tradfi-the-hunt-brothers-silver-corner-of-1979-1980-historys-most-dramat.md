## The Setup: Why the Hunts Chose Silver

Nelson Bunker Hunt and Herbert Hunt were Texas oil billionaires who became convinced in the late 1970s that the United States was heading toward monetary collapse. Their solution was to acquire as much physical silver as possible as a hedge against dollar debasement   a thesis that was not wrong in its logic, only extreme in its execution.

Beginning in 1973, the Hunts began accumulating silver futures and physical silver. By 1979, they and their Saudi partners collectively controlled approximately 130 million ounces of silver   nearly half of the world's entire non-monetary silver supply. The fundamentally sound inflation hedge thesis had become a corner.

## The 1979-1980 Blow-Off: $6 to $50

As the Hunts accumulated, silver prices rose steadily from $6 to $16 during 1979. This rise attracted more speculative buying as traders recognized the concentration of bullish demand. By January 1980, the feedback loop had driven silver to $49.45 per ounce on January 18, 1980.

At this peak, the gold-silver ratio had compressed to approximately 17:1   meaning silver was extraordinarily expensive relative to gold by any historical standard. The first warning sign.

## The Collapse: COMEX Rule Changes and Margin Calls

The COMEX exchange changed its rules in January 1980, dramatically raising margin requirements and restricting new silver futures positions to liquidation-only. This rule change (called "Silver Rule 7") immediately ended the Hunts' ability to maintain their position. Unable to meet margin calls as prices fell and unable to buy new contracts to hold prices up, the Hunts faced forced liquidation.

Silver fell from $50 to $10 within two months. "Silver Thursday" (March 27, 1980) saw the Hunts unable to meet a $100 million margin call. The collapse was complete.

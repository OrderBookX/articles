## The Dollar Cycle: Historical Patterns

Academic analysis of DXY (the dollar index) over the post-Bretton Woods era (1973-present) identifies three complete cycles: a strengthening cycle from 1978-1985 (culminating in Plaza Accord intervention), a weakening cycle from 1985-1995, a strengthening cycle from 1995-2001 (US tech boom), a weakening cycle from 2001-2011 (deficits, China growth, commodity boom), and a strengthening cycle from 2011-2022 (US growth divergence, eventual rate hikes).

## The Four Drivers of Dollar Cycle Turning Points

Turning points in dollar cycles are driven by four factors that historically align simultaneously at major reversals:

1. Interest rate differential reversal: the Fed moves from hiking to cutting while other central banks are still tightening. This reduces the dollar's yield advantage   the primary fundamental driver of the 2022 dollar strength.

2. Current account dynamics: US current account deficit widening (more dollars flowing abroad for imports) is associated with dollar weakening.

3. Global growth divergence reversal: when non-US economies begin growing faster than the US, capital flows toward non-US assets, weakening the dollar.

4. Risk appetite shift: when global risk appetite moves from defensive (favoring dollar safe-haven) to offensive (favoring emerging market and commodity currencies), the dollar typically weakens.

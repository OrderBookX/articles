## Gold Mining's Energy Cost Structure

A typical gold mine's cost structure breaks down approximately as follows: energy (diesel for mining equipment, electricity for processing) accounts for 15-20% of total cash costs. Labor accounts for 25-35%. Materials (chemicals, explosives, tires) account for 15-20%. Sustaining capital accounts for 15-25%.

When oil prices rise 30% (diesel is the primary fuel for haul trucks and generators), a 20% energy cost share means all-in sustaining costs rise by approximately 6% (20% × 30% oil price increase). At an AISC of $1,100/oz, this adds approximately $66/oz to production costs   reducing the profit margin and, at extreme oil prices, making marginal mines uneconomical.

## The Mine Shutdown Threshold and Its XAUUSDT Implication

When gold prices fall toward industry AISC levels (currently approximately $1,200-1,400/oz for the global average), marginal mines approach economic breakeven. Some begin curtailing production or shutting down temporarily. The relationship between oil prices and this floor is dynamic: if oil prices rise significantly (adding to mining AISC), the production shutdown threshold rises in gold price terms   meaning gold must fall to a higher absolute price before supply responses occur. Conversely, when oil prices fall sharply (as in 2014-2016), mining costs decline and mines can remain profitable at lower gold prices, pushing the natural supply response floor lower.

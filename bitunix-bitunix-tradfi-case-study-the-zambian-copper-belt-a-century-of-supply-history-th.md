## The 1970s Nationalization: When Political Risk Became Real

In 1969, Zambia's first post-independence president Kenneth Kaunda nationalized 51% of the copper mines under the banner of economic sovereignty. The Zambian government's logic was straightforward: why should foreign companies extract and profit from Zambia's primary natural resource?

The nationalization's practical effect on copper production was severe. Mine management expertise left with the foreign operators. Reinvestment fell as the government diverted copper revenues to social spending and prestige projects. By the 1980s, Zambian copper production had fallen from over 700,000 tonnes annually (its 1970s peak) to below 500,000 tonnes. The physical evidence of what happens when political risk materializes in a major copper-producing country was written into the decade-long production decline that followed.

## The 1990s Privatization and Its Reversal

Economic crisis and IMF pressure led Zambia to privatize the copper mines in the late 1990s, selling to Vedanta, Anglo American, and other international operators. The privatization initially led to mine closures (unprofitable operations at then-low copper prices) and mass unemployment, creating social and political pressure that would shape Zambian copper policy for the next decade.

This cycle of nationalization, decline, privatization, and renewed political pressure repeats across copper-producing developing countries and is ongoing today in the DRC (Democratic Republic of Congo) and Peru.

## Where Aluminum Substitution Is Already Occurring

High-voltage transmission lines: aluminum conductor steel-reinforced (ACSR) wire already dominates long-distance high-voltage transmission globally because aluminum's lower weight-to-conductivity ratio makes it technically superior for long spans. Copper is used primarily in lower-voltage distribution systems (the "last mile" to homes and buildings) where flexibility, corrosion resistance, and connection reliability matter more than weight.

EV charging cables: the higher-power Level 3 fast-charging cables face a trade-off between copper (smaller diameter, more flexible) and aluminum (lighter, cheaper). Several charging equipment manufacturers are developing aluminum-based DC fast charging cables that could reduce copper content per charging station by 30-40%.

## Where Copper Cannot Be Substituted

Electric motor windings: copper's electrical conductivity is fundamentally superior to aluminum at the same cross-sectional area, and the space constraints in EV motor designs make the higher conductivity of copper non-substitutable without significant motor redesign. Motors represent one of the largest copper-per-vehicle applications in EVs.

PCB and semiconductor interconnects: the miniaturization required at the chip level makes copper the only practical conductor. No aluminum substitution is occurring at the electronics level.

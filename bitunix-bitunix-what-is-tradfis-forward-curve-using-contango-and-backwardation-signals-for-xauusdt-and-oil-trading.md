What is TradFi's forward curve, and why does its shape matter more than the spot price for positioning decisions? The forward curve shows the price of a commodity across different delivery dates, from spot (immediate delivery) through near-term and long-dated futures contracts. The shape of this curve, whether it slopes upward (contango) or downward (backwardation), provides critical intelligence about current physical supply-demand conditions that spot price alone cannot reveal.

## What Is TradFi's Contango and What Does It Signal?

What is TradFi's contango? A market is in contango when futures prices are higher than spot prices. For example, if spot gold is $2,100 and the 12-month gold futures are $2,160, the market is in contango by approximately 2.9%. Normal contango represents the cost of carry: storage, insurance, and financing costs for physically holding a commodity forward.

When contango is steeper than normal carry costs, it signals that near-term physical supply is adequate or oversupplied: the market does not urgently need metal right now. For XAUUSDT, this is typically a neutral to slightly bearish short-term signal because it indicates physical buyers are not competing aggressively for immediate delivery.

## What Is TradFi's Backwardation and What Does It Signal?

What is TradFi's backwardation? A market is in backwardation when futures prices are below spot prices. If spot copper is $4.00/lb and 6-month copper futures are $3.85/lb, the copper market is in backwardation. Backwardation signals physical supply tightness: buyers urgently need copper now and are willing to pay a premium for immediate delivery over deferred delivery.

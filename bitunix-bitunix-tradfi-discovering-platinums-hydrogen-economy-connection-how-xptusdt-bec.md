## The Fuel Cell Discovery

Hydrogen fuel cells use platinum as the catalyst in the membrane electrode assembly   the core component that converts hydrogen into electricity. Each fuel cell requires approximately 30-60 grams of platinum per 100 kilowatts of output. For a hydrogen fuel cell electric vehicle, this means 10-30 grams per car depending on the power requirements.

At first glance, this seems small compared to platinum's traditional automotive catalyst demand. But the comparison changes when you look at the trajectory. Hydrogen fuel cell passenger vehicle production was 3,000-5,000 units globally in 2019. By 2023, it had grown to 20,000+ units. Heavy-duty hydrogen trucks   where fuel cells make more economic sense than batteries for long-haul transport   are being deployed commercially by Toyota, Hyundai, and Chinese manufacturers at an accelerating rate.

But the truly transformative application is stationary power: PEM electrolyzers that use platinum to split water into hydrogen for industrial energy storage. When European energy policy in 2022 committed to 40 gigawatts of electrolyzer capacity by 2030, the platinum demand implied by that commitment was more significant than all other demand growth scenarios combined.

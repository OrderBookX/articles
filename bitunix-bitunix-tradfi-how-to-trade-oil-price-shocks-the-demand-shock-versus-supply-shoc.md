## Demand Shock Characteristics and Their Implications

A demand shock drives oil prices higher through economic activity improvement. Characteristics:

Simultaneous with rising PMI: when PMI new orders and production sub-indices are rising at the same time as oil prices, demand is pulling up prices.

Accompanied by rising refinery utilization: EIA data shows refineries operating at high utilization rates (above 93%), meaning they are actively converting crude to products for strong end-user demand.

Accompanied by rising crack spreads: product demand is strong enough that refiners are paying more for crude while charging more for products   the entire demand chain is tight.

Accompanied by falling crude inventory: EIA weekly crude stocks are declining because refiners are using it.

Demand-shock response: build bitunix oil futures positions at full conviction size with the standard holding period (6-10 weeks). The demand shock will persist as long as the underlying economic activity sustains it.

## Supply Shock Characteristics and Their Implications

A supply shock drives oil prices higher through reduced availability of crude. Characteristics:

Without rising PMI: economic activity is flat or declining at the same time oil prices rise   stagflation risk.

With declining refinery utilization: refiners are reducing throughput because high crude prices are compressing refinery margins   the demand chain is weakening while supply is reduced.

With falling crack spreads or unchanged: product demand is not pulling   only supply is pushing crude prices up.

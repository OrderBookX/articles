## The Refinery Throughput Signal Chain

The oil market signal chain, from upstream to downstream: crude oil production → crude inventory → refinery throughput → product inventory → product prices (crack spreads) → final consumer demand.

The most analytically useful link is the middle of this chain: when product inventories (gasoline, distillates) are declining and product prices are rising (crack spreads widening), refiners are incentivized to maximize throughput   pulling more crude out of inventory and through their systems. This physical demand for crude at the refinery level is the most direct predictor of crude inventory draws.

EIA tracks product-level inventories separately: gasoline inventory (most important for summer driving season demand), distillate inventory (diesel and heating oil, most important for winter and industrial demand). Weekly releases alongside the crude inventory data.

## The Seasonal Product Market Calendar

US gasoline demand follows a strong seasonal pattern: demand peaks during summer driving season (Memorial Day through Labor Day, approximately late May to early September). Gasoline inventories build through winter and draw through summer. When gasoline inventories are falling ahead of schedule (before Memorial Day) relative to 5-year seasonal average, the early-season demand is stronger than expected   a leading signal for crude demand.

Distillate/diesel demand peaks in winter (heating oil demand) and during harvest season (agricultural equipment). When distillate inventories are falling faster than seasonal average in October-November, heating demand is stronger than expected   another leading crude demand signal.

## The Container Rate vs Air Freight Divergence Signal

When container rates are falling (sea freight prices declining) while air freight rates are rising (air cargo demand increasing), a specific signal emerges: the goods being shipped urgently by air are time-sensitive (electronics, pharmaceuticals, fashion)   high-value goods where buyers are willing to pay air freight premiums. When air freight is rising, businesses are restocking high-value inventory rapidly   a leading consumer demand signal.

The divergence between slow/cheap (sea) and fast/expensive (air) freight creates a split signal: container rates reflect the bulk of merchandise trade, while air freight reflects the premium urgent portion. When both are rising, global trade is broadly accelerating.

## The COVID-Lesson: Disruption vs Demand Signals

The 2021-2022 container rate spike to historic highs was driven by port congestion and supply chain disruption   not by demand growth alone. Container rates spiking due to port congestion (supply constraint) while actual trade volumes are flat is a different signal than rates rising due to genuine trade volume growth. Cross-check container rate changes with PMI new export orders sub-components to distinguish demand-driven from disruption-driven rate moves.

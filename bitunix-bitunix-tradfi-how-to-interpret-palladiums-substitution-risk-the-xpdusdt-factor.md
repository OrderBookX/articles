## The Chemistry of Substitution: Why It Is Technically Feasible

Palladium and platinum are adjacent elements in the platinum group metal family with similar electron configurations. Both can catalyze the oxidation reactions (converting CO and hydrocarbons to CO2 and H2O) and reduction reactions (converting NOx to N2) required in gasoline automotive catalysts.

The historical reason palladium replaced platinum in gasoline catalysts during the 1990s-2000s: at the time, palladium was significantly cheaper than platinum and demonstrated superior performance at the lower temperatures typical of modern fuel-injection gasoline engines. As palladium prices have risen dramatically   from parity with platinum in 2010 to a peak of 3x platinum's price in 2022   the economic incentive for reverting to platinum in gasoline catalysts has grown.

## The Technical Challenges That Slow Substitution

Full platinum substitution in gasoline catalysts requires an automotive-grade engineering change. Automakers must: validate platinum's performance in their specific engine architecture across all temperature ranges, obtain regulatory certification for the modified catalyst formulation (EPA, Euro 6, China 6 testing), and update their manufacturing supply chains.

This validation and certification process typically takes 3-5 years from research initiation to commercial deployment. Major automakers began seriously investigating platinum-for-palladium substitution when palladium reached 2x platinum's price in 2019. The earliest large-scale commercial deployment in new vehicles would be 2024-2026   and will be confirmed in Johnson Matthey's annual PGM report when actual automotive loadings data changes.

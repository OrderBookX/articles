## The Fundamental Difference Between WTI and Brent

WTI is a physically delivered futures contract for light sweet crude oil delivered at Cushing, Oklahoma   the US domestic oil pricing hub. Its price reflects US domestic supply-demand balance, particularly US shale production levels and Cushing storage utilization.

Brent is a physically settled benchmark for North Sea crude that serves as the primary pricing reference for approximately 60-70% of globally traded crude oil. Because most international crude oil (OPEC exports, West African crudes, Russian Urals) is priced relative to Brent rather than WTI, Brent is the global oil price benchmark.

## The WTI-Brent Spread: What It Reveals

The spread between WTI and Brent (WTI minus Brent) is typically negative (Brent premium) or near zero. When WTI trades at a premium to Brent (a positive spread), US domestic supply is relatively tight compared to global supply   a specific signal about US domestic conditions. When Brent trades at a large premium to WTI (large negative spread), international supply is tighter than US domestic supply   typically driven by OPEC cuts affecting non-US producers more than US shale.

The significance for bitunix oil futures practitioners: when the WTI-Brent spread widens sharply, the fundamental driver of oil prices has shifted from global supply-demand balance to US-specific supply dynamics. Monitoring the spread identifies which analytical framework (OPEC/global or EIA/US-domestic) is currently the primary price determinant.

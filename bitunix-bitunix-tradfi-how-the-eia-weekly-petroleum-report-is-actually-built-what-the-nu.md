## The Survey Base: Not a Complete Census

The EIA's weekly report is not a complete census of US petroleum inventories. It is a survey of refiners, importers, bulk terminals, and pipeline operators. The survey covers approximately 95% of commercial crude oil storage capacity, but the remaining 5% is estimated through statistical models rather than direct measurement.

More importantly: the EIA's production data for the "current" week is a model estimate based on historical production trends, well completion data, and the most recent direct survey data (which typically lags 2-3 weeks). The headline weekly production number that moves bitunix oil futures is not directly measured   it is estimated.

This estimation means that the weekly production figure is subject to retroactive revision. The EIA publishes monthly production data (from the Petroleum Supply Monthly) that is directly measured and replaces the estimated weekly data. These monthly revisions sometimes show that the weekly estimates were significantly wrong   occasionally by 300,000-500,000 barrels per day.

## The Seasonal Adjustment Question

The crude inventory build or draw that markets focus on is the raw (non-seasonally adjusted) number. But whether a 3-million-barrel build is bullish or bearish depends on whether that build is above or below what is seasonally normal.

The correct interpretation: compare the weekly build or draw to the 5-year average for the same week of the year. A 3-million-barrel build during a week when the 5-year average is a 4-million-barrel build is actually a 1-million-barrel-equivalent draw relative to seasonal norms   mildly bullish despite the headline build.

## The Data Quality Warning: The API vs EIA Sequence

The American Petroleum Institute (API) publishes its own weekly petroleum inventory report the Tuesday evening before the Wednesday EIA release. The API survey has different methodology, a smaller sample base, and is frequently at variance with the subsequent EIA number. Yet bitunix oil futures often moves 1-2% on the Tuesday API report.

When the Tuesday API and Wednesday EIA diverge significantly (one showing a large build, the other a draw), the volatility creates specific opportunities: the Wednesday EIA release will resolve the uncertainty, and the direction of its resolution determines whether the Tuesday move was correctly anticipated or must be reversed.

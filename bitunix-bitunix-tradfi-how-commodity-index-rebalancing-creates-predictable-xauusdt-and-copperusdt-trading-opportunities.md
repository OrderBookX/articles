## How Commodity Index Rebalancing Works

Index providers set target weights for each commodity in their benchmarks (for example, BCOM targets approximately 8-9% gold, 5-6% copper, 28-30% energy in aggregate). Commodity prices drift relative to each other throughout the year, causing actual weights to deviate from targets. Annual rebalancing brings weights back to targets by selling overweight commodities and buying underweight commodities.

The timing of the rebalancing creates predictable price pressure: if gold has appreciated more than other commodities during the year, it is likely overweight in BCOM, meaning index rebalancing will require selling gold to reduce its weight. This index rebalancing selling creates temporary downward price pressure on overweight commodities and upward pressure on underweight commodities.

## Identifying Overweight and Underweight Commodities Before Rebalancing

BCOM publishes its target weights and current weights regularly. By tracking commodity performance relative to these targets throughout the year, traders can identify which commodities are becoming overweight (candidates for index selling) and which are becoming underweight (candidates for index buying) before the actual rebalancing occurs.

Practical approach: in November-December before the typical January index rebalancing, calculate which major commodities have significantly outperformed or underperformed their BCOM target weights. Overperformers by more than 2-3 percentage points relative to target are likely candidates for rebalancing selling; underperformers are candidates for buying.

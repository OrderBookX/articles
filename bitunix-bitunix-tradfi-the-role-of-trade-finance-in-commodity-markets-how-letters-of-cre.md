## What Trade Finance Is and Why It Matters

A typical physical copper trade between a South American mine and a Chinese smelter works as follows: the Chinese buyer issues a Letter of Credit (LC) through its bank, guaranteeing payment to the seller. The LC is backed by the buyer's credit line with the bank. The seller (mining company or trader) uses the LC to access financing before the physical copper arrives.

When banks restrict LC issuance   either because of counterparty credit concerns, general risk aversion, or regulatory capital constraints   buyers lose the financing mechanism that makes large commodity purchases possible. A Chinese smelter that cannot get LC financing cannot complete large copper purchases even if it wants to.

## The 2008 and 2020 Trade Finance Crises

The clearest historical examples of trade finance disruption affecting commodity markets: in September-October 2008, LIBOR spiked and interbank lending froze. Trade finance LCs became expensive or unavailable. Global commodity trade volumes fell sharply for several weeks   not because demand had collapsed but because the financing mechanism had broken down. Physical copper and oil moved less than supply-demand fundamentals justified simply because the payment guarantee infrastructure was temporarily impaired.

In March 2020, the same dynamic occurred briefly before massive central bank intervention restored liquidity.

## The Monitoring Signal: TED Spread and ICC Trade Finance Report

The TED spread (3-month LIBOR minus 3-month T-bill yield, or its SOFR-based equivalent) measures interbank lending stress. When the TED spread rises above 0.5% for two consecutive weeks, trade finance conditions are tightening across the banking system.

The ICC (International Chamber of Commerce) Annual Trade Finance Report provides comprehensive data on LC issuance volumes, rejection rates, and regional trade finance availability. Published annually (free at iccwbo.org), it provides the longer-term structural picture of whether trade finance availability for commodity-importing countries is improving or deteriorating.

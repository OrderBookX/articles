## The Monthly Review Protocol

## What to Look for in a Review

Your review should answer three questions. Is my plan still executing on schedule? Has my average cost moved in a direction I expected given market conditions? Has anything fundamentally changed about the assets in my plan? If the plan is running, your average cost is behaving as expected, and your thesis is intact, there is nothing to change. Close the spreadsheet and come back next month.

## Red Flags That Require Action

Only a small set of conditions should trigger plan modifications. An asset in your plan has undergone a fundamental deterioration like a major security breach, team collapse, or regulatory ban. Your financial situation has changed and you can no longer sustain the current allocation. You have reached a predetermined exit target. Price movements alone are never a red flag. They are normal market behavior.

## The Danger of Daily Checking

Checking your portfolio daily during a bear market creates a cumulative negative emotional experience that erodes your commitment. If Bitcoin drops 2% per day for a week, daily checking forces you to process seven separate negative data points. Monthly checking presents one aggregated data point that is much easier to process rationally. Frequency of observation directly correlates with emotional interference. Academic research on investor behavior confirms that more frequent portfolio monitoring leads to more frequent trading, which leads to worse returns. The optimal monitoring frequency for a long-term DCA plan is monthly at most.

## Tools for Maintaining Distance

Delete price widgets from your phone home screen. Unfollow crypto price accounts on social media. Turn off exchange notification alerts except for security-related messages. Create as much friction as possible between your daily life and your portfolio awareness. The less you observe, the less you interfere, and the better your results.

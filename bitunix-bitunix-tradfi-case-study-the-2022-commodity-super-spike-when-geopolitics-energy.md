## The Three Converging Forces

Force one was the COVID recovery demand surge. By early 2022, the global economy had largely reopened. Consumer demand was running above pre-COVID levels in most major economies, supported by the accumulated savings from lockdown periods and sustained fiscal stimulus. Commodity demand was rising across the board.

Force two was the energy transition investment bottleneck. Solar panel production, EV manufacturing, and grid infrastructure were all scaling simultaneously, competing for the same copper, aluminum, silver, and other materials. Supply chains were not ready for this simultaneous scaling.

Force three was Russia's invasion of Ukraine. On February 24, 2022, Russia launched its full-scale invasion. Russia supplies 40% of global palladium, 10-15% of global platinum, significant percentages of nickel and copper, 12% of global oil exports, and was the primary natural gas supplier to Europe. The simultaneous potential disruption of multiple commodity supply chains in one military event was unprecedented.

## The Peak and Partial Reversal: What Happened

By March 2022, nickel had doubled in a week on the LME (triggering the historic short squeeze). Palladium was above $3,000. Oil was above $130. Copper was above $4.90. Wheat prices hit 14-year highs. The simultaneous spike across all commodities reflected the three forces compressing at once.

The partial reversal that followed was not a fundamental resolution   it was the market recognizing that at these prices, demand destruction was beginning. $130 oil was destroying European industrial demand faster than the Russia supply disruption was removing supply. The demand destruction provided temporary price relief even as the fundamental supply constraints persisted.

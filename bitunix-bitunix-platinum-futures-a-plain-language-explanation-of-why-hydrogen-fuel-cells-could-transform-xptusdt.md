## How a Hydrogen Fuel Cell Actually Uses Platinum

A hydrogen fuel cell generates electricity by combining hydrogen fuel with oxygen from the air. Inside the fuel cell, a platinum catalyst on a thin membrane accelerates the chemical reaction that splits hydrogen molecules into protons and electrons. The electrons flow through an external circuit as electricity; the protons combine with oxygen to produce water as the only byproduct.

The platinum catalyst is essential because without it, the reaction between hydrogen and oxygen would proceed too slowly at the temperatures required for practical vehicle use. Platinum's unique catalytic properties make it the most effective and currently irreplaceable catalyst material for proton exchange membrane fuel cells, the type used in fuel cell vehicles and increasingly in stationary power applications.

Each proton exchange membrane fuel cell stack in a vehicle uses approximately 30-60 grams of platinum. At $1,000 per ounce, that represents $1,000-$2,000 worth of platinum per vehicle in the fuel cell stack alone.

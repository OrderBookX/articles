## The Gold Standard Era and Its End

From 1944 to 1971, the Bretton Woods system fixed the US dollar to gold at $35 per ounce, and all other major currencies were fixed to the dollar. This system created monetary stability but required that the US Federal Reserve maintain dollar credibility by limiting money supply expansion to available gold reserves.

In August 1971, President Nixon suspended dollar-to-gold convertibility, effectively ending the gold standard. This event, known as the Nixon Shock, freed central banks globally to expand money supply without gold constraint. The direct result: gold, no longer constrained to $35 per ounce, appreciated to $850 per ounce by 1980 as the monetary system absorbed the shock.

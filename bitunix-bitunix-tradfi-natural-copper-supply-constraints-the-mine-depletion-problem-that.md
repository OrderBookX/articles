## The Ore Grade Decline Reality

Copper ore grade   the percentage of copper in the rock being mined   has been declining steadily for decades across most major mining regions. Average ore grades in Chile, the world's largest copper producer, have fallen from approximately 1.7% copper content in 1999 to approximately 0.7% in 2023. This means miners must process 2.4x more rock to produce the same amount of copper as 25 years ago.

The consequences: energy consumption per tonne of copper produced has risen by approximately 40% over the same period (more rock to crush and process). Water consumption per tonne has also risen significantly, creating particular challenges in Chile's arid Atacama region where water scarcity already constrains operations. Labor productivity has declined as operations move to less accessible ore bodies.

## The All-In Sustaining Cost Implication

Declining ore grades combined with higher energy and water costs have driven industry AISC from approximately $0.90/lb in 2007 to approximately $2.60-2.80/lb in 2023. The implication: the copper price required for marginal mine profitability has risen 3x over 16 years, meaning the floor below which copper prices cannot sustainably fall has risen significantly.

This creates a structural price floor that is independent of Chinese demand cycles: even in a global recession, copper prices cannot remain below the AISC floor for extended periods without triggering mine shutdowns that reduce supply until price recovers.

## New Mine Development: The 10-Year Problem

From discovery to production, a new copper mine typically takes 10-15 years to develop. Exploration activity, environmental impact assessment, community consultation, permitting, engineering, construction, and commissioning all add time. The most significant large copper discoveries made in the 2010s (Resolution in Arizona, Tampakan in the Philippines) are still not in production in 2024 due to regulatory and community opposition.

The pipeline of new copper mines that could enter production between 2025 and 2030 is insufficient to meet projected demand growth from electrification and existing consumption. The supply gap must be filled by either higher prices (incentivizing more development) or demand restraint (economic recession). The higher-prices path is the base case in virtually every institutional copper supply analysis.

## Why the Gold Pool Was Necessary

Under the Bretton Woods system, other currencies were pegged to the dollar and the dollar was convertible to gold at $35. When private market gold demand consistently exceeded $35 (because everyone could see that US monetary expansion was making $35 increasingly undervalued), central banks had to sell gold from their reserves to maintain the $35 price.

The Gold Pool's mechanics: when gold in the London market rose above $35.20, the Pool's managers would sell gold from reserves to cap the price. When it fell below $34.80, they would buy. The operation successfully maintained $35 gold from 1961 to 1967.

In the week of March 11-15, 1968, the Pool sold $2.5 billion worth of gold in five trading days   exhausting what the US Treasury was willing to sacrifice. France had already withdrawn from the Pool in 1967. The remaining seven central banks closed the London gold market for two weeks, and when it reopened, the Gold Pool was dissolved. Gold immediately traded to $44 in the private market   a 26% premium to the now-defunct official price.

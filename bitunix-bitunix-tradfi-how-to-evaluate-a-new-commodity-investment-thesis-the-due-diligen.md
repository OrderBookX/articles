## The Six-Question Due Diligence Framework

Question 1: Is there a tradeable instrument with sufficient liquidity?

A genuine commodity thesis requires a liquid instrument to express it. The bitunix tradfi pairs suite includes the major commodity derivatives. For emerging commodities (lithium, cobalt) not yet available as direct perpetuals on major exchanges, the thesis can only be expressed through proxy instruments (copper as an EV battery proxy, specific mining equities). Verify the specific instrument before building analytical work around a thesis.

Question 2: Is the primary demand driver identifiable and measurable?

Every tradeable commodity thesis requires a specific, measurable primary demand driver. For copper it is Chinese PMI new orders. For gold it is TIPS yields. For an emerging commodity thesis, identify: what specific data series measures the demand that the thesis is based on? If the answer is "it's a narrative" rather than a measurable data series, the thesis is not yet analytically tractable.

Question 3: Is the supply side analyzable?

Demand thesis alone is insufficient   supply determines how much of the demand growth translates into price appreciation. Can the supply side be analyzed using publicly available mine production data, AISC estimates, and development pipeline information? For established commodities (copper, gold, oil), yes. For emerging commodities, supply data quality varies significantly.

Is the demand theme near-term (6-18 months), medium-term (2-5 years), or long-term (5-10 years)? Match the timeframe to the appropriate instrument structure. Near-term themes can be expressed through perpetual futures with defined entry/exit. Long-term themes (hydrogen economy, rare earth nationalism) may be better expressed through equity positions or options if available.

Question 5: What is the consensus view and how crowded is it?

When Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, and Bloomberg News all have essentially the same thesis simultaneously, the thesis is typically at least partially priced in. The analytical edge comes from having a perspective that differs from consensus   either in timing, magnitude, or mechanism. If the new thesis is identical to the prevailing institutional consensus, there is less alpha available from adding it.

Question 6: What would make this thesis wrong?

Before committing to a new analytical framework, identify the specific conditions that would falsify it. A thesis that cannot be falsified is an untestable narrative, not an analytical framework.

## The BDTI and Its Oil Demand Signal

The BDTI rises when: crude oil exports are high (more oil being shipped = more tanker demand), importing country demand is strong (refineries drawing down reserves and replacing them), and tanker supply is constrained (new tanker deliveries slow, scrapping is high).

The BDTI falls when: crude exports are reduced (OPEC cuts reducing the oil that needs to be shipped), importing demand is weak (refineries not buying), or tanker capacity is oversupplied.

Historical analysis shows the BDTI leads crude oil prices by approximately 2-4 weeks with moderate reliability: when the BDTI rises for three consecutive weeks while crude oil prices are flat, the physical shipment demand is building ahead of price recognition.

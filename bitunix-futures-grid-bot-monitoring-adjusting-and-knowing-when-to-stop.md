Futures Grid Bot — Monitoring, Adjusting, and Knowing When to Stop

Launching a grid bot isn't the finish line — it's the starting point of active management. The bot handles execution, but you handle strategy. And strategy requires monitoring.


## Daily Monitoring

Check your bot dashboard at least once daily. It takes five minutes and can save your capital. The three numbers that matter: grid profit (realized gains from completed cycles), unrealized PnL (current open position value), and total PnL (the sum of both). If grid profit is growing but total PnL is declining, the market is trending against your direction. You need to decide: hold and wait for reversal, or stop and cut losses.


## When to Reconfigure

Market structure shifts are the primary reason to reconfigure. If the consolidation pattern that justified your grid has broken — new support or resistance levels, a clear breakout with volume — your old range no longer reflects reality. Running a grid in a range that no longer exists is guaranteed loss accumulation.

Adjusting means stopping the current bot and creating a new one with updated parameters. You cannot modify a running grid's range or grid count on most platforms, including Bitunix. This is by design — it ensures execution consistency. Stop, reassess, and relaunch with parameters that match the new market structure.


## Knowing When to Stop

Knowing when to stop requires honesty. If your bot has been paused for days because price left the range, and the trend shows no signs of reversing, holding costs you in unrealized loss and opportunity cost. Stop the bot, realize the loss, and redeploy capital where conditions are favorable.

Also consider seasonal and event-driven timing. Before major economic announcements, protocol upgrades, or historically volatile periods, it may be wise to stop running bots entirely and wait for conditions to stabilize.

When stopping a bot, also consider the current funding rate environment. If you're holding a large unrealized position at the time of stopping, and funding is about to settle, you may pay one additional funding fee during the market close execution. On liquid pairs this is usually negligible, but on smaller pairs with volatile funding rates, timing your stop before the next funding settlement can save a small but unnecessary cost.

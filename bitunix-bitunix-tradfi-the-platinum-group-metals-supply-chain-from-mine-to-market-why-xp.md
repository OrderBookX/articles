## The South African Platinum-Palladium Supply Chain

South Africa's Bushveld Igneous Complex is the world's largest known deposit of platinum group metals, containing an estimated 80% of global platinum reserves and 50% of palladium reserves. Mining this deposit involves underground mining at depths of 1,000-2,500 meters, followed by ore processing through milling, flotation concentration, and smelting.

The critical infrastructure dependency: South African PGM smelting requires vast quantities of electricity. Eskom, South Africa's state power utility, has experienced recurring load-shedding (rolling blackouts) due to aging infrastructure, maintenance backlogs, and financial mismanagement. When Eskom implements Stage 4-6 load-shedding (4-6 hours of outages daily), PGM smelters are forced to reduce throughput. A 20% reduction in smelter throughput for 30 days removes approximately 150,000-200,000 ounces of combined PGM production   a 1-2% impact on annual global supply that is significant for XPTUSDT.

## The Russian Norilsk Supply Chain for Palladium

Russia's Norilsk region in Siberia produces palladium as a co-product of nickel mining. The mining-to-refining-to-export chain: ore is mined at the Norilsk mines, processed at the Norilsk smelter (the world's largest smelter by volume of metal processed), refined at dedicated PGM refineries, and exported through Arctic shipping lanes.

The logistics dependency: Norilsk's Arctic location means the export chain depends on ice-breaking shipping during winter months. When weather conditions are severe enough to restrict Arctic shipping, short-term palladium export delays create temporary XPDUSDT tightness that dissipates when shipping resumes.

## How the Bullwhip Effect Works in Copper Markets

When an end-use manufacturer (say, an automotive company assembling EVs) experiences an unexpected demand surge, the following supply chain amplification sequence occurs:

Level 1 (Manufacturer): Orders more copper wire and components than immediate needs, building a safety buffer against future shortages. Order size: demand + 30% buffer.

Level 2 (Component Fabricator): Sees the manufacturer's inflated order and also builds a safety buffer. Order size to copper refinery: demand + 30% buffer + another 30% fabricator buffer.

Level 3 (Copper Refinery): Sees the fabricator's inflated order and forwards the amplified signal to the mine or LME trader. The initial 20% demand increase has become a 70%+ increase at the mine level due to the compounding safety buffer effect.

The result: LME spot copper demand and price spike far beyond what the underlying end-use demand justifies. When the buffers are eventually liquidated (all three levels simultaneously reduce orders to burn through excess inventory), LME copper demand and price fall far below what underlying end-use demand would justify.

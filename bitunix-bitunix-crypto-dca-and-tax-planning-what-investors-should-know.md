## Every Purchase Is a Taxable Event in Many Jurisdictions

In most countries that tax cryptocurrency, each purchase creates a cost basis record. When you eventually sell, the difference between your purchase price and sale price determines your gain or loss. With DCA, you have dozens or hundreds of individual purchase records, each at a different price. This creates what is known as multiple tax lots.

## Why DCA Makes Record Keeping More Complex

A single lump-sum purchase gives you one cost basis to track. A weekly DCA plan running for a year gives you 52 different cost bases. Over four years, that is 208 entries. ## Cost Basis Methods

Different jurisdictions allow different methods for calculating which shares you are selling. Common methods include FIFO (first in, first out), LIFO (last in, first out), and specific identification. The method you use can significantly impact your tax liability. Consult a tax professional to determine which method is permitted and optimal in your jurisdiction, as rules vary significantly between countries. FIFO sells your oldest, typically cheapest, shares first, potentially creating larger gains. Specific identification lets you choose which lots to sell, offering more control over your tax outcome.

## The Advantage of DCA for Tax Loss Harvesting

Because DCA purchases happen at many different price points, some of your lots will inevitably be purchased at higher prices than the current market. During downturns, you can sell these specific lots at a loss to offset gains elsewhere in your portfolio. This can meaningfully reduce your tax bill in years where you have realized significant gains from other investments. This strategy, called tax loss harvesting, is more accessible with DCA because you have many lots at many prices to choose from.

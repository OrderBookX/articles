## Traditional Futures: The Foundation of TradFi Derivatives

Traditional commodity futures contracts are legally binding agreements to buy or sell a specific quantity of a commodity at a specific price on a specific future date. COMEX gold futures, for example, commit the buyer to purchase 100 troy ounces of gold at the agreed-upon price on the contract's expiration date.

In practice, most futures traders do not intend to take or make physical delivery. They close their positions before expiration by taking the opposite trade. The futures price moves in line with the spot price plus carrying costs (interest rates, storage costs, and convenience yield), and the profit or loss from closing before expiration is the difference between entry and exit futures prices.

Traditional futures require a regulated brokerage account, have minimum contract sizes that can be $100,000 or more in nominal terms, and expire on specific dates requiring active management to avoid the complexity of physical delivery.

## Options: The Third Layer of TradFi Derivatives

Options give the buyer the right, but not the obligation, to buy (call option) or sell (put option) an asset at a specific price before a specific date. Options have value from two components: intrinsic value (how much in-the-money they are) and time value (how much time remains before expiry for the trade to work out).

Options allow expressing directional views with defined maximum loss (the premium paid) and theoretically unlimited upside. The Bitunix exchange focuses on perpetual contracts rather than options, but understanding options mechanics helps traders interpret option-based market signals like implied volatility that affect commodity market sentiment.

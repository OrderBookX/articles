## The Two-Market Price Dislocation Effect

When a major economy imposes tariffs on another's exports, the immediate effect is to split what was a unified global commodity market into two differently-priced segments:

Domestic price (within the tariff wall): higher than the pre-tariff price because imported supply is restricted or more expensive. US steel tariffs created higher US steel prices while global steel prices remained lower.

External price (outside the tariff wall): lower than the pre-tariff price because some supply that would have gone to the tariffed market is redirected to other markets, creating oversupply. EU steel prices fell slightly as some Asian steel that previously went to the US sought European buyers instead.

For copper specifically: when the US has considered copper import tariffs (periodically discussed for national security reasons), the potential effect would be higher US copper prices (since significant US copper consumption is met by imports) while LME prices (the global benchmark that COPPERUSDT tracks) might temporarily weaken as supply redirected away from the US seeks other buyers.

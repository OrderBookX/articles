## What the Crack Spread Measures

The "3-2-1 crack spread"   the most commonly tracked version   represents the approximate profit from refining three barrels of crude oil into two barrels of gasoline and one barrel of distillate (diesel/heating oil). The formula: (2 × gasoline price + 1 × diesel price - 3 × crude oil price) / 3.

When this crack spread is high (refiners are profitable), refineries run at maximum capacity and buy more crude oil   increasing crude demand. When the crack spread is narrow or negative (refineries are losing money), they cut throughput and reduce crude purchases   decreasing crude demand.

This creates a leading indicator relationship: crack spread compression (falling refinery margins) precedes EIA inventory builds because refineries reduce crude purchases immediately when margins deteriorate, but the EIA data shows the resulting lower crude intake with a 1-2 week lag.

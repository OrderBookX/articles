## The Essential Reading List

1. "Hot Commodities" by Jim Rogers (2004): Rogers' accessible account of the first commodity supercycle and the fundamental case for commodity investing. Not technically sophisticated but establishes the macro perspective on commodity cycles that frames multi-year analysis.

2. "The Big Short" by Michael Lewis (2010): Not about commodities   about how a small group of practitioners identified a massive structural mispricing and profited from it. The intellectual lesson: how to identify when fundamental analysis diverges from market consensus and have the conviction to act on the divergence.

3. "Market Wizards" series by Jack Schwager (four books): interviews with the most successful commodity and futures traders of the 1980s-2010s. The behavioral and operational patterns that create sustained success are more consistent across these practitioners than any specific analytical method.

4. "Against the Gods: The Remarkable Story of Risk" by Peter Bernstein (1996): the history of human understanding of probability and risk. Essential for understanding why position sizing and risk management are not optional adjuncts to analytical frameworks but the mathematical foundation beneath them.

5. "The Alchemy of Finance" by George Soros (1987): Soros' original presentation of reflexivity theory   how market participant beliefs affect the fundamentals they are analyzing, creating feedback loops. Directly relevant to how COT positioning extremes create the conditions for their own reversal.

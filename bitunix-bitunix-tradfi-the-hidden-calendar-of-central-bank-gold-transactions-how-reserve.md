## The Reporting Timeline and Its Information Gap

Central bank gold transactions are reported to the International Monetary Fund through the International Reserves template   a monthly submission with a 30-day reporting lag. This means a purchase made on the first of any month will not appear in official statistics until approximately 60 days later.

The practical information gap: when China's PBOC reports a 10-tonne gold purchase, the purchase actually occurred 30-60 days before the announcement. The physical gold market   specifically the London physical gold market where central banks typically transact   will have already absorbed the demand. The market impact occurred at the time of purchase, not at the time of reporting.

## The Forward Signals: Where Central Bank Buying Appears Before Official Reports

Three data sources provide advance signals of central bank gold accumulation before official reports:

London Bullion Market Association monthly data: the LBMA publishes monthly statistics on the volume of gold cleared through London's settlement system. When clearing volumes are significantly above seasonal norms, large institutional transactions (potentially including central bank purchases) are occurring. This data publishes approximately 4-5 weeks after month-end   before the 60-day official reserve report.

BIS quarterly data on central bank foreign reserve composition: the Bank for International Settlements publishes aggregate data on global reserve composition changes with a 1-quarter lag. When the aggregate "gold" line increases significantly, central bank buying is confirmed at a systemic level.

SGE (Shanghai Gold Exchange) premium and volume data: when Chinese demand through the SGE is running significantly above historical norms, PBOC accumulation (conducted through SGE or related channels) may be contributing.

## The Policy Decision Framework That Precedes Accumulation

Central bank reserve managers typically operate on annual allocation review cycles. Policy decisions to increase gold allocation (increasing from 5% to 8% of reserves, for example) are made by the reserve management committee and implemented over 6-18 months of gradual accumulation   not through single large transactions that would move markets.

This means the market signal from a single reported central bank transaction is not "they bought today" but "they made a policy decision months ago to increase allocation and are currently implementing it."

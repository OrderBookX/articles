## The Common Supply Factor: South African and Russian Concentration Risk

Platinum and palladium share an extraordinary supply concentration. South Africa produces approximately 75% of global platinum and 35% of global palladium. Russia produces approximately 40% of global palladium and smaller but meaningful platinum quantities. Together, two politically complex countries control the overwhelming majority of primary platinum group metals production.

This shared supply concentration means that South African mining disruptions (power crises, labor strikes, operational accidents) and Russian geopolitical events affect both metals simultaneously. When either country faces supply disruption risk, both XPTUSDT and XPDUSDT benefit from supply premium appreciation. This common factor justifies treating them as a basket for supply disruption positioning.

## The Divergent Demand Factors: Where the Relative Value Comes From

While supply concentration creates a common floor, demand drivers for platinum and palladium are diverging over time due to automotive technology transitions. Palladium is predominantly used in gasoline vehicle catalytic converters and faces long-term demand headwinds from EV adoption. Platinum is used in both gasoline and diesel catalytic converters, and has a growing role in hydrogen fuel cell applications that creates long-term demand optionality.

This demand divergence, combined with the historical relationship between their prices, creates the relative value opportunity: when palladium trades at a significant premium to platinum (as it did from 2018-2022), the case for long XPTUSDT and short XPDUSDT is strong because the demand headwinds for palladium are greater than for platinum.

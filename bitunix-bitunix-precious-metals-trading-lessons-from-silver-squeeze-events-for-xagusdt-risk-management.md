## Anatomy of a Silver Squeeze: How They Start and Why They End

Silver squeezes begin when coordinated buying, whether from institutional funds, retail communities, or both, absorbs available physical and futures market supply faster than new supply can be sourced. Because the silver market is smaller and less liquid than gold, even modest coordinated demand can overwhelm near-term supply capacity.

The mechanics: as futures prices rise, holders of short positions (including commercial hedgers who legitimately sell futures to lock in production prices) face increasing margin calls. Margin calls force short-covering purchases that further drive prices up, creating a feedback loop. When the squeeze reaches its peak, the mechanism reverses as futures exchanges raise margin requirements (making it more expensive for longs to hold positions), forced long liquidation begins, and the price collapses back toward fundamental levels.

The 2021 WallStreetBets silver squeeze attempted to replicate this mechanism through coordinated retail buying but was less successful than the 1979-1980 Hunt Brothers squeeze because the retail coordination was fragmented and physical silver supply quickly became available from alternative sources, containing the price spike.

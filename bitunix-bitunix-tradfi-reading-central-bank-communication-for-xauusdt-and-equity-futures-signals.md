## The Fed Communication Hierarchy

Federal Reserve communication has a defined hierarchy of authority and market impact:

FOMC statement (highest impact): The eight-sentence summary released at each FOMC meeting, following a precise template that changes incrementally to signal policy evolution. Any change from the prior statement is highly intentional and carries significant signaling weight.

Chair press conference (high impact): Extemporaneous responses to reporter questions that provide more nuanced guidance than the formal statement. Watch for qualifying language: "if appropriate," "in coming meetings," "data dependent" signal uncertainty; "firmly committed," "without hesitation," "clear" signal conviction.

Fed governor speeches (medium impact): Individual FOMC voting members communicate through speeches and interviews between meetings. Voting member speeches (especially vice chairs and New York Fed president) carry more weight than non-voting members.

Fed minutes (medium impact): Released three weeks after FOMC meetings, minutes show the range of opinions within the committee. When minutes reveal more hawkish internal discussion than the public statement suggested, markets react as the information is digested.

## The Four Major Gold Price Regimes in Modern History

Regime 1   The Gold Standard and Bretton Woods Era (1944-1971): gold fixed at $35/oz. No analytical framework needed   the price was set by government policy. The lesson: when government policy creates artificial price constraints, the eventual liberation creates explosive price discovery.

Regime 2   The First Free Market Era (1971-1980): Nixon closes the gold window in August 1971, freeing gold to trade at market prices. Gold rose from $35 to $850   a 2,329% appreciation. The drivers: unprecedented peacetime inflation (Nixon-era expansionary fiscal policy), negative real rates, oil price shocks (OPEC 1973 and 1979), and the psychological shock of the dollar's gold backing being removed. The analytical lesson: when inflation exceeds nominal rates by large margins for extended periods, gold's real return advantage creates substantial appreciation.

Regime 3   The Long Bear Market (1980-2001): gold fell from $850 to $250 over 21 years. The drivers: Paul Volcker's aggressive rate hikes restored positive real rates (gold's primary headwind), the dollar strengthened dramatically in the early 1980s, and inflation expectations normalized. The analytical lesson: persistently positive and rising real rates create sustained gold bear markets regardless of any other supportive factors.

Regime 4   The Modern Bull Markets (2001-Present): Three distinct bull periods: 2001-2011 (negative real rates from Greenspan/Bernanke stimulus, Chinese demand growth, TIPS yields near or below zero); 2015-2020 (ECB and BOJ negative rates, global real rate decline); 2018-2023 (COVID-response monetary expansion, TIPS yields reaching -1.7%).

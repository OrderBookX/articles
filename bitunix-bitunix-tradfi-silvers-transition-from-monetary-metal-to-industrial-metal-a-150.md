## The 19th Century: Silver as Money

The Coinage Act of 1792 established the United States on a bimetallic standard: both gold and silver were legal tender, with a fixed ratio of 15:1 silver to gold by weight. In the 1870s, most major economies adopted the gold standard, demonetizing silver. The resulting reduction in monetary silver demand contributed to falling silver prices through the late 19th century.

The political consequences were enormous: American farmers and debtors who owed fixed debts in appreciated dollars became the silver movement's constituency. William Jennings Bryan's 1896 "Cross of Gold" speech is the most famous political expression of the monetary silver debate. Silver's 19th century history is fundamentally monetary   it was money, and its price reflected monetary policy decisions rather than industrial demand.

## The 20th Century Transition: Photography and Electronics

By the mid-20th century, silver had found massive new industrial applications. Photographic film required silver halides: at the industry's peak in the 1990s, photography accounted for 30% of all annual silver consumption. The electronics revolution of the 1970s-2000s created a new application: silver contacts, conductors, and solders in every consumer electronic device.

As photography declined (digital cameras replaced film), the silver demand that had been absorbed by Kodak, Fuji, and Agfa was replaced by electronics and, subsequently, solar panels.

## The Mechanical Dollar Effect on Each Instrument

Gold (XAUUSDT): Gold is the most directly inversely correlated major asset to the dollar. Because gold is priced globally in dollars, a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive in all other currencies   reducing international demand and pushing prices down. The historical correlation between DXY and XAUUSDT on a rolling 12-month basis is approximately -0.65. The dollar is gold's second most important driver after real rates.

Copper (COPPERUSDT): Copper has a moderate inverse dollar correlation (approximately -0.40 to -0.50). The mechanism: a stronger dollar increases the cost of copper for international buyers (especially Chinese importers who buy in dollars but have RMB-denominated revenues), reducing demand. Additionally, dollar strength often coincides with global growth concerns that reduce Chinese manufacturing activity independently.

Oil (bitunix oil futures): Oil has significant but variable inverse dollar correlation (approximately -0.30 to -0.45). The correlation is weaker than gold because oil's supply-demand balance often dominates price direction regardless of dollar movements. OPEC production decisions can offset dollar headwinds.

Silver (XAGUSDT): Silver has a dollar correlation intermediate between gold and copper   reflecting its dual monetary-industrial nature. Approximately -0.50 to -0.55.

Platinum group metals (XPTUSDT, XPDUSDT): Weaker dollar correlation (approximately -0.25 to -0.35) because automotive industry cycles dominate their price action more than monetary factors.

Every OPEC+ member country has a fiscal break-even oil price   the oil price at which its government budget is balanced. When the market price falls below the fiscal break-even, the government is running a deficit and faces growing pressure to either cut spending (politically difficult) or pump more oil (in violation of the cartel agreement but producing more revenue). ## The Fiscal Break-Even Table and Its Compliance Signal

Annual IMF Article IV consultations (free at imf.org) publish fiscal break-even oil price estimates for major oil exporters. Approximate 2024 estimates:

Saudi Arabia: approximately $80-85/barrel. When Brent oil is above $85, Saudi Arabia's fiscal position is comfortable and compliance with production cuts is sustainable. When Brent falls below $75, Saudi Arabia faces growing fiscal pressure.

Iraq: approximately $95-100/barrel. Iraq's fiscal break-even is among the highest in OPEC due to high government spending commitments. Iraq faces the highest fiscal pressure from lower oil prices and has historically been the OPEC member most likely to exceed its quota when prices are insufficient to cover government costs.

UAE: approximately $60-65/barrel. Abu Dhabi's sovereign wealth fund provides fiscal buffer   UAE can sustain compliance even at lower oil prices without fiscal stress.

Russia: approximately $65-70/barrel (pre-2022 estimate; sanctions and oil price cap complicate post-2022 analysis). Russia's fiscal flexibility depends significantly on the actual price received for its Urals blend exports, which trades at a discount to Brent.

Nigeria: approximately $100-110/barrel. Among the highest fiscal break-even in OPEC. Nigeria's oil production has also been hampered by infrastructure theft and underinvestment, making it difficult to produce at quota even when it wants to comply.

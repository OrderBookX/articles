## The First Question That Changed Everything

My first serious attempt at understanding gold led me to a single question that opened the entire framework: if gold pays no interest, why would anyone hold it instead of a bond? The answer   that the relevant comparison is to bonds after inflation, meaning real interest rates   cracked the code. When real rates are positive, bonds genuinely outperform gold. When real rates are negative, gold genuinely outperforms bonds. The TIPS yield on the Federal Reserve's FRED database became the first thing I checked every week.

I spent four weeks just watching TIPS yields and XAUUSDT prices simultaneously. The relationship was not perfect but it was unmistakable. Every time TIPS yields fell, gold either rose or held steady. Every time TIPS yields rose sharply, gold fell. I had my primary driver.

## Building the Framework: One Driver at a Time

The second driver I added was the dollar (DXY). Gold is priced in dollars globally, so a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for everyone outside the US and reduces international demand. I set up a simple weekly check: is the DXY direction the same as the TIPS yield direction? When both confirmed bearish dollar and declining real rates, the setup quality was highest.

Third was the COT report   the weekly Commitment of Traders data showing how speculative funds are positioned in gold futures. I learned the percentile normalization technique: not the raw number but where the positioning sits in its 5-year historical range. When the COT was below the 30th percentile (light speculative positioning), there was room for funds to add longs. Above the 80th percentile, there was crowding risk.

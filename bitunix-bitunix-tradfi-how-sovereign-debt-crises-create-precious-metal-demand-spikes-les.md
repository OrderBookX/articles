## The Three Transmission Channels

Channel 1   Monetary expansion: When a government cannot finance its debt through taxation or bond issuance, the central bank monetizes it (prints money to buy government bonds). This expands the money supply, raises inflation expectations, and lowers real rates   the direct gold-supportive transmission from sovereign debt to monetary conditions.

Historical example: Weimar Germany 1921-1923. The most extreme modern example of hyperinflation driven by debt monetization. Gold (and hard assets generally) preserved purchasing power through the entire hyperinflation period.

Channel 2   Safe-haven demand surge: When a significant sovereign default occurs (Argentina 2001, Greece 2010-2012, Lebanon 2020), domestic citizens and international investors simultaneously seek safe-haven assets. Gold benefits as a globally recognized, government-independent store of value that cannot be restructured, defaulted on, or confiscated through the same mechanisms as sovereign bonds.

Channel 3   Currency devaluation: Sovereign debt crises typically involve significant currency devaluation as the government inflates away debt. Gold's price rises in the devalued currency, creating local-currency appreciation that attracts more domestic buyers   reinforcing the safe-haven demand surge.

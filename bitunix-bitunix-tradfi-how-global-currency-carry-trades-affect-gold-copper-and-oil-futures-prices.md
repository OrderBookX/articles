## What Is the Currency Carry Trade and How Big Is It?

In the currency carry trade, investors borrow in currencies with very low interest rates (historically the Japanese yen and Swiss franc) and invest in currencies or assets with higher yields (emerging market bonds, commodity-linked currencies, US stocks). At peak activity, these trades involve trillions of dollars of positions.

The commodity market connection: when carry trades are expanding, the dollar funding used often flows into commodity-linked emerging market currencies like the Australian dollar, Brazilian real, and Chilean peso. This EM currency strength reflects capital flowing into commodity economies, supporting the economic activity that drives copper, oil, and agricultural commodity demand.

## Carry Trade Unwind: The Commodity Headwind Phase

Carry trade unwinds occur suddenly when market stress or central bank policy shifts make the yen (or other funding currencies) appreciate rapidly. When the yen strengthens sharply, carry traders are forced to sell their commodity and EM currency positions to repay yen borrowings. This forced selling creates sharp, rapid commodity price declines that are not driven by fundamental supply-demand changes.

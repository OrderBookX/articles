## The Four Functions of a Bank Commodity Desk

Function 1   Client facilitation: Banks execute large commodity transactions for institutional clients (hedge funds, corporate hedgers, pension funds, central banks) at bid-ask spreads. This client flow gives the bank real-time positioning intelligence   they know whether their largest institutional clients are net buyers or sellers before the rest of the market does.

Function 2   Market making: Banks provide continuous buy and sell quotes to the market, earning the bid-ask spread as compensation for providing liquidity. Market making creates specific price smoothing effects: banks absorb large one-directional client orders by temporarily warehousing the risk on their own book, then gradually distributing it back to the market over days to weeks.

Function 3   Proprietary trading: Until the Volcker Rule (2010) limited proprietary trading for US banks, investment bank prop desks were major directional commodity traders. Today, US bank prop trading in commodities is more limited, but non-US banks (including European and Asian institutions) maintain larger prop commodity operations.

Function 4   Structured product manufacturing: Banks create commodity-linked structured products for clients (reverse convertibles, commodity notes, guaranteed return products) that require specific hedging activities. When a bank sells a commodity-linked structured product that pays off if gold rises above $2,500, the bank buys the corresponding options or futures as a hedge   creating specific demand at specific strike levels.

## The Option Expiry Effect on XAUUSDT

The options book that banks maintain creates the "max pain" phenomenon: around major options expiry dates (the last Friday of each month for COMEX options), banks have a financial incentive to see the price settle near the strike price where the maximum notional value of options expires worthless   known as the "max pain" strike.

## The Scrap Market Structure

Copper scrap is classified by purity grade: Number 1 copper (clean copper wire and tubing, above 99% copper content), Number 2 copper (mixed lower-grade material, 96-98% copper), and yellow brass (copper-zinc alloy scrap from demolition and manufacturing). Each grade trades at a discount to the LME cathode price   the discount (the "spread") reflects processing costs required to refine scrap back to cathode-equivalent quality.

When LME copper prices rise sharply, the economics of scrap collection improve   collectors earn more per pound recovered. This price elasticity means scrap supply rises with LME prices (unlike mine supply, which cannot respond quickly). The scrap supply response partially moderates the physical tightness that high LME prices reflect.

The practical implication for COPPERUSDT: during acute LME price spikes (above 20% in 60 days), rising scrap supply provides a partial price ceiling effect over the following 8-12 weeks as scrap processors accelerate deliveries. The scrap supply response does not prevent structural tightness from persisting, but it moderates the speed of price appreciation.

## The China Scrap Import Policy Signal

China is the world's largest copper scrap importer. Chinese government scrap import policies   which have tightened and loosened over time based on environmental regulations   directly affect the volume of scrap available for Chinese smelters to process. When China tightens scrap import standards, Chinese copper supply from secondary sources falls, increasing Chinese mine and cathode import demand   a COPPERUSDT bullish supply constraint. When China loosens scrap standards, secondary supply expands, partially substituting for mine supply.

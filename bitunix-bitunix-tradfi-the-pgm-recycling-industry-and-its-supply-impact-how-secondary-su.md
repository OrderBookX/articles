## How the Autocatalyst Recycling Chain Works

When a gasoline vehicle reaches end of life (typically 12-15 years after manufacture), the catalytic converter is removed before the vehicle is scrapped. Specialist recycling companies (the largest include Johnson Matthey, Umicore, and BASF) process the converters to recover the platinum, palladium, and rhodium they contain.

The metal recovery process: converters are shredded, sampled, and processed through a smelting and chemical refining chain that extracts PGMs from the ceramic substrate. The final output is refined sponge platinum, palladium, and rhodium that enters the market as secondary supply.

The key variable: the volume of end-of-life vehicles reaching scrapyards in any given year. Vehicle scrappage follows a predictable demographic curve: cars manufactured 12-15 years prior are the primary recycling feedstock. Cars manufactured during the 2007-2012 period (with their specific PGM loadings, which were higher than modern vehicles due to less efficient catalyst technology) represent the current primary recycling source.

## The Loading Factor: Why Recycling Supply Is Growing

Modern automotive emission standards require higher PGM loadings per vehicle than standards from 15-20 years ago. This means that as cars manufactured in 2010-2015 (with their higher loadings) reach end of life in 2024-2030, the per-vehicle PGM recovery from recycling will be higher than the per-vehicle recovery from vehicles scrapped in 2018-2022.

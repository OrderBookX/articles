The copper-gold ratio   the price of copper divided by the price of gold   is one of the most widely followed cross-commodity signals in global macro analysis. The ratio's logic is simple but powerful: copper is primarily an industrial metal (rising when growth is strong, falling when it weakens) while gold is primarily a monetary metal (rising when monetary uncertainty is high, falling when monetary conditions normalize). The ratio captures the market's simultaneous assessment of both economic growth and monetary conditions.

## What the Copper-Gold Ratio Actually Measures

When the copper-gold ratio is rising (copper outperforming gold), the market is simultaneously saying: economic growth expectations are improving (bullish copper) AND monetary fear is declining (bearish gold's safe-haven premium). This combination   better growth, less monetary stress   is the definition of a risk-on macro environment.

When the copper-gold ratio is falling (gold outperforming copper), the market is simultaneously saying: economic growth expectations are deteriorating (bearish copper) AND monetary uncertainty is rising (bullish gold's monetary premium). This is the definition of a risk-off macro environment.

## The US Treasury Yield Connection

The copper-gold ratio has a historically tight correlation with 10-year US Treasury nominal yields. The logic: when economic growth is strong and monetary fear is low (high copper-gold ratio), investors sell safe-haven bonds, pushing Treasury yields higher. When growth is weak and monetary fear is high (low copper-gold ratio), investors buy safe-haven bonds, pushing Treasury yields lower.

This correlation   copper-gold ratio as a leading indicator for Treasury yields   has been observed in academic research (notably by Jeffrey Gundlach of DoubleLine Capital) over 30-year periods.

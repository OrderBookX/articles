## What Lease Rates Measure and Why They Matter

When demand for borrowed physical metal (from mining companies hedging production, from industrial users needing immediate supply, or from investment banks supporting various financial structures) exceeds the available supply of lendable metal, lease rates rise. High lease rates indicate physical tightness: there is more demand for immediate physical supply than supply available for lease.

This physical tightness, when sustained over several weeks, typically leads to appreciation in spot and futures prices as market participants compete for available physical metal and hedge forward risk.

Conversely, very low or negative lease rates (metal being offered cheaply for lease) indicate physical surplus: supply significantly exceeds current demand for borrowing. This typically precedes price weakness or sustained sideways action.

## Gold Lease Rate Monitoring (GOFO and Libor-Minus-Lease)

The Gold Forward Offered Rate (GOFO) historically measured the difference between gold lease rates and dollar interest rates. While GOFO is no longer published by the LBMA (discontinued in 2015), the implied lease rate can be calculated from gold swap rates available through financial data platforms.

When implied gold lease rates turn negative (meaning borrowers are being paid to borrow gold), the physical gold market is in surplus. When they spike sharply positive, physical tightness is developing. Monitoring LBMA published gold forward prices and calculating the implied lease from them provides this signal with a weekly update frequency.

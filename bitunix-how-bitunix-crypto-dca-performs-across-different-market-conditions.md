## DCA in a Bull Market

During a sustained uptrend, each new purchase is typically more expensive than the last. Your average cost rises with each cycle. In this environment, a lump-sum investment at the start would have outperformed DCA because prices only went up from the initial entry. However, nobody knows in advance that a bull market will continue without significant pullbacks. DCA in a bull market still accumulates units and builds your position. It just does so at progressively higher costs. The trade-off is lower risk of catastrophic timing versus slightly lower overall returns compared to perfect lump-sum entry.

## DCA in a Bear Market

This is where DCA shines. As prices decline, each purchase buys more units for the same dollar amount. Your average cost drops with every cycle. When the market eventually recovers, all those cheap units amplify your returns. Bear market DCA is uncomfortable in real time because your portfolio value keeps declining even as you keep buying. But mathematically, it is the most favorable environment for dollar cost averaging. The units you accumulate during these periods become the foundation of your future returns when the cycle turns.

## DCA in a Sideways Market

Range-bound markets produce the most consistent DCA results. Prices oscillate within a band, and your purchases naturally capture both the highs and lows of that range. Your average cost converges toward the midpoint of the range. There is no dramatic outperformance or underperformance. Just steady, predictable accumulation.

## The Full Cycle Perspective

Real markets do not stay in one condition permanently. Over a multi-year period, you will experience all three environments. A DCA plan running through a complete cycle captures the averaging benefit across all conditions. Bear market purchases provide the cheap units. Bull market appreciation provides the returns. Sideways periods provide stability.

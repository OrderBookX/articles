## The Quarterly Stress Test Methodology

Step 1   Define three extreme scenarios relevant to the current portfolio. Examples:

Scenario A   2008-style global financial crisis: simultaneous 30% XAUUSDT decline, 40% COPPERUSDT decline, 50% TSLAUSDT decline, 60% MSTRUSDT decline, 10% CRCLUSDT decline. All correlations go to 1.0 in Phase 1.

Scenario B   1980-style rate shock (unexpected Fed tightening): TIPS yields spike 150bp in three months. XAUUSDT falls 20%, MSTRUSDT falls 35%, TSLAUSDT falls 25%, COPPERUSDT mixed, bitunix oil futures may rise (demand maintained, real rate shock).

Scenario C   China hard landing: COPPERUSDT falls 35%, bitunix oil futures falls 20%, XAUUSDT initially falls then recovers. TSLAUSDT falls 40% (GF3 exposure). XPDUSDT falls 25%.

Step 2   Apply each scenario to the current portfolio composition. Calculate the total account loss under each scenario for the current open positions at current sizes. Does any scenario produce a loss exceeding the maximum annual drawdown limit (15%)? If yes, position sizing is too large.

Step 3   Identify the worst-case scenario for the current portfolio. Which scenario produces the maximum loss? Is it the scenario most likely to occur given current macro conditions? If the worst-case scenario is also the most likely (CMSI is already elevated, the primary concern is the rate shock and rates have been rising for weeks), reduce positions before the scenario materializes.

## The Stress Test Action Thresholds

If any stress scenario produces greater than 20% account loss: reduce total position size until the maximum stress scenario loss is below 20%. This is an absolute limit   not a guideline.

If the worst-case scenario produces 15-20% account loss: this is within the maximum annual drawdown limit but warrants monitoring. Add to the next quarterly review as a priority assessment.

If all three stress scenarios produce less than 15% account loss: current portfolio sizing is within acceptable tail risk parameters.

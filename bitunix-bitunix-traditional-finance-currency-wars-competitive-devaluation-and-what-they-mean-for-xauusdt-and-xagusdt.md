## What Are Currency Wars and When Do They Occur?

Currency wars emerge when major economies believe their trading partners are gaining unfair advantage through deliberate currency weakening. The classic cycle: Country A's central bank cuts rates more aggressively than peers, weakening its currency. Country B's exports become less competitive and its central bank responds by cutting rates to weaken its own currency. Country A then cuts further, and the cycle of competitive devaluation begins.

The 2010-2012 "first currency war" of the post-financial-crisis era, the 2015-2016 competitive devaluation round (including China's surprise yuan devaluation), and the post-2020 era of coordinated global monetary expansion all created currency war conditions where gold appreciated substantially because it could not be devalued by any central bank's decision.

## Identifying Currency War Conditions: The Three Signals

Signal 1: Multiple G10 central banks cutting rates simultaneously or significantly expanding balance sheets within the same 6-month period. This synchronized easing indicates competitive devaluation dynamics rather than independent policy responses.

Signal 2: Explicit government statements about currency levels and competitive concerns. When finance ministers and central bank governors publicly discuss exchange rate competitiveness (as opposed to inflation and employment mandates), currency war dynamics are politically active.

Signal 3: Accelerating decline in the trade-weighted exchange rates of multiple major currencies against real assets (commodities, gold). When the dollar, euro, and yen are all weakening against gold simultaneously, the competitive devaluation dynamic is clearly manifesting in market prices.

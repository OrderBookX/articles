**Student:** Every time I try to trade bitunix oil futures, I feel like I'm playing a geopolitical news lottery. A headline about Saudi Arabia appears and oil jumps 3%. Then a headline about US production hits and it falls 2%. How do I build a framework around something this news-driven?

**Mentor:** The news-driven appearance is partly real and partly an illusion. Yes, oil reacts to individual headlines. But the price level at which oil settles after the news reaction is determined by fundamentals: supply-demand balance, inventory levels, and Saudi willingness to defend specific price floors. Understanding those fundamentals tells you whether a news spike is sustainable or noise.

**Student:** Where do I start with the fundamentals?

**Mentor:** The EIA Weekly Petroleum Status Report, published every Wednesday at 10:30 AM US Eastern time. It tells you: US crude production (rising = bearish supply), crude imports (rising = either rising demand or strategic buying), refinery utilization (falling = less crude demand), commercial crude stocks (rising = oversupply developing), and Cushing Oklahoma inventory (rising above seasonal norms = storage filling, bearish).

**Student:** Which of those matters most?

**Mentor:** The commercial crude inventory trend over 4-6 consecutive weeks. A single week's build or draw can be distorted by weather, holidays, or pipeline disruptions. Four to six consecutive weeks of builds creates a genuine oversupply signal; four to six consecutive draws creates a genuine tightness signal. The trend, not the weekly number, is the trading signal.

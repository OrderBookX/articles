## The By-Product Dominance Problem

Approximately 70-75% of annual silver mine supply is a by-product of gold, copper, lead, and zinc mining. This means silver producers are not primarily in the business of producing silver   they are producing other metals, with silver as an incidental output. The supply response when silver prices rise is therefore muted: a copper miner that produces silver as a by-product will not significantly increase copper production simply because silver prices are high. The silver supply is largely inelastic to silver prices.

The implication: silver supply cannot increase rapidly in response to price signals the way gold mine supply can when prices rise. A 50% increase in silver prices would produce only a modest 5-10% increase in primary silver mine supply (from pure-silver mines) and essentially zero increase in by-product supply. This supply inelasticity is a structural long-run argument for silver's price volatility relative to its own cost of production.

## Recycling: The Price-Elastic Secondary Supply

Silver recycling   the recovery of silver from photographic film, electronics, jewelry, and silverware   provides approximately 15-20% of annual supply. Unlike gold recycling (which responds primarily to jewelry holders selling at high prices), silver recycling is increasingly dominated by industrial scrap recovery. As silver prices rise, electronics recycling margins improve, and more industrial scrap is processed for silver recovery.

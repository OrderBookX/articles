## Why Oil Is Denominated in Dollars and What It Means

When Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and other major oil exporters sell oil, they receive US dollars. These petrodollars must then be recycled   invested in US Treasury bonds, US equities, or other dollar-denominated assets. This recycling creates sustained demand for US financial assets from oil-exporting countries, which has historically supported the dollar and contributed to lower US interest rates than would otherwise prevail.

The implication: when oil prices are high and stable (GCC countries running large surpluses), the recycling flow into US financial assets supports the dollar   partially offsetting the typical inverse dollar-commodity correlation. The dollar weakens against oil-importing currencies but the petrodollar recycling creates a dollar-supportive flow from oil-exporting countries.

## The De-Dollarization of Oil Trade and Its Signal

Since 2022, a gradual but meaningful shift has occurred: China and Russia have been settling some bilateral oil trade in yuan (CNY) and rubles rather than dollars. Saudi Arabia has accepted yuan for some Chinese oil purchases. India has settled some Russian oil in rupees.

The aggregate scale remains small relative to total global oil trade, but the directional shift has been consistent: when more oil is settled in non-dollar currencies, less petrodollar recycling flows to US financial assets   marginally reducing the dollar-supportive flow that the petrodollar system historically created.
